FOR THE TEAM · ASYNC

Talking points · and how to leave feedback

Read at your own pace. Drop questions or thoughts in the GitHub thread at the bottom — we'll fold them into the design as we go. No need to wait for a meeting.

  1. What we're building. A step inside Velocity where the rep picks the right contract Type using Keitha's 3-question wizard, and Velocity pre-fills the customer's details. Reps see a near-ready draft instead of a blank Word doc.
  2. Keitha's wording stays hers. We're not rewriting the four templates — we just mark which bits change per customer (names, dates, prices).
  3. Keitha & Mal stay in control. Templates live inside Velocity; they can open and edit any time. Next deal uses the new version. No engineering bottleneck.
  4. Reps can tweak a single deal's copy without touching the master. If Dalila asks to remove the IP bit again, the rep does it for her deal; the master is untouched.
  5. Signed contracts go where people need them. Velocity keeps a copy AND pushes the signed PDF to a shared Google Drive folder per customer — Lauren, Ricky, anyone with Drive access can find it. (Fixes the NZIE / Storepro pain.)
  6. Signing — external for now. Rep generates the PDF, sends via DocuSign / Dropbox Sign, uploads the signed copy back. Velocity pushes to Drive. One-click in-portal signing comes later.
  7. Each Type gets its own scope shape. Consulting reads differently from a build, which reads differently from a subscription. Four shapes, not one (see Part 3 of the mockup).
  8. Coreshift first, GP held for v1.2. Same engine — drops in when Mal's product reset is ready.
  9. Roles. Super-admin: Abe + Ricky. Template author (across both companies): Keitha + Mal. Reps just use templates.
  10. Where we are. In design discussion — no code shipped yet. Build kicks off once the two open questions below are settled.
Two things we'd most like your view on:
  • One document or two? GP's examples combine proposal + contract in one signable thing. Do we want Coreshift the same — one signature — or proposal first, contract following?
  • Which of the two GP examples is the "flimsy" one? (Keitha) — so we model the good one, not the bad one.

3 · The 4 scope shapes

One scope section per Type. They share the cover, cover letter, "How we work together", investment schedule, Terms and signature blocks (as partials) — but the scope section reflects what kind of engagement it actually is.

1

Business Consultation

Pure advisory. Nothing built. SOW-driven.
  1. Engagement objective(s) What problem are we solving
  2. Scope of Work — this engagement Bulleted deliverables for this SOW
  3. Approach Workshops / sessions / reviews · cadence
  4. Timeline & milestones Typical: 4–12 weeks, dated milestones
  5. Outputs What the customer ends up with (report, plan, model)
  6. Out of scope What we won't touch in this SOW
2

Bespoke Build

Custom build. Customer owns it. Milestone payments.
  1. Project objective What we're building, in plain words
  2. Solution overview What it does · high-level shape
  3. Deliverables Concrete artifacts (app, workflow, tool)
  4. Milestones & payments 30 / 40 / 30 (or as agreed)
  5. Acceptance criteria What "done" means
  6. Warranty 60 days post-acceptance (per Terms)
  7. Ongoing support (optional) Refers to SLA annexure if taken
  8. Out of scope
3

Platform Subscription

Customer subscribes to a platform we own. Example: Noozey.
  1. Platform overview What they're getting access to
  2. Customisations for this customer Anything specific to them
  3. Onboarding Setup, training, data import
  4. Ongoing service Support tier · release cadence
  5. Data ownership Their data, their control — friendly recap
  6. Roadmap What's coming next that they'll benefit from
  7. Out of scope
4

Platform Resale

Customer onsells access to their End Users. Example: Key Content.
  1. Platform overview What they're licensing to resell
  2. End User model How they package it for their End Users
  3. Pricing structure Per-End-User pricing · how it scales
  4. Customer's responsibilities Onboarding, billing, support, ToS for End Users
  5. Coreshift's responsibilities Platform uptime, releases, security
  6. Onboarding (the customer themselves) Setup of their reseller environment
  7. Reporting End User metrics shared back to them
  8. Out of scope
